Publications & Presentations
A survey of southern hemisphere meteor showers
One of the primary authors in paper published in the Journal of Planetary and Space Sciences. DOI: 10.1016/j.pss.2018.02.013
Meteor showers identify streams of meteoroids that approach from a similar direction and presumably originated from the same parent object. In recent years, over 300 meteor showers have been identified, of which 112 have been established and are certain to exist. A working list of identified showers is maintained by the IAU Meteor Data Center. The southern hemisphere meteor showers are not as well mapped as those on the northern hemisphere. We report here on results from a CAMS-type video-based meteoroid orbit survey (Jenniskens et al., 2011) conducted in New Zealand in 2014–2016. The capability of the technique was demonstrated in earlier small scale video-based meteoroid orbit surveys in the southern hemi- sphere (e.g., Jopek et al., 2010; Molau and Kerr, 2014). The newly detected showers are compared to recent results from a southern hemi- sphere radar orbital survey conducted with the Southern Argentina Agile MEteor Radar (SAAMER) (Pokorny et al., 2017). It is found that video-based and radar-based observations detect, in general, different streams.
Artificial intelligence techniques applied to automating meteor validation and trajectory quality control to direct the search for long-period comets
Presented at the International Meteor Conference 2017 by Marcelo de Cicco.
We describe an effort to automate the CAMS (Cameras for All-sky Meteor Surveillance) data re- duction pipeline using artificial intelligence techniques to discriminate meteors from other types of detections and to determine correct solutions during triangulation. The effort will make it possible to have the results from a night of low-light video observations available to the observers the following day. As part of the data reduction pipeline, meteors are classified as real and assigned to showers. Results are presented in such a way that each shower can be identified, and new showers from the occasional encounter with the dust trails of long-period comets can be recognized. The detection of such rare showers will allow to direct the search for long-period comets in dedicated deep surveys.
Searching for Long-Period Comets with Deep Learning Tools
Contributor. Presented at the Workshop on Deep Learning for Physical Sciences (at the Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems) by Susana Zoghbi.
In planetary defense, long-period comets are recognized as the potentially most devastating threat. However, any new comet on an impact trajectory would likely only be discovered about one year before impact. The goal of this project is to add years of extra warning time by providing comet searchers directions on where to look for comets when they are still far out. To aid and guide a dedicated search for these dangerous objects, meteor showers may offer a clue. Comets leave debris trails as they travel along their orbits. When our planet intersects such debris trails, we see them as meteors. By detecting rare aperiodic meteor showers from dust clouds, we can estimate the orbit of the parent body and narrow down the search space where to look for long-period comets. We developed deep learning tools that allow such automation. Specifically, we developed a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that discerns images of meteors vs. other objects in the sky and achieves precision and recall scores of 88.3% and 90.3%, respectively. In addition, we developed a Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) network that encodes the light curve tracklets into a latent space, and learns to predict whether the tracklet corresponds to a meteor or not. The LSTM achieves a precision of 90.0% and a recall of 89.1%. These methods can now be used by meteor astronomers to automatically analyze sky detections and help guide the search for long-period comets.
Artificial Intelligence Strategic Vision: A Framework for Augmenting Science at NASA
One of the primary authors in white paper submitted to NASA
New eras in science and exploration call for new tools and new perspectives. By taking novel approaches to problems old and new, and by using innovative and proven methods, great steps in scientific progress are made. Perhaps no field lends itself as well to this role in scientific progress in the current age as artificial intelligence (AI). Given that AI is not as abstract as it is often perceived to be, there are potential applications to research being done at NASA. By exploring uses in industry and academia, connections to research and analysis, project management, and space missions are made, encouraging further investment in AI and interaction with the AI community by NASA. The value of AI is evidenced by previous work, having proved to be useful as well as economic. Advances in science and technology are being made using AI, so as leader in making scientific progress, NASA should continue to increase its involvement in the field. This means including AI experts on projects, hosting seminars and events, and using the work that has already been done in the field. By taking these steps, new things could be achieved, and current work could be done better, more quickly, and more efficiently.
Mammographic Density and Its Association to Genetic Risk
Breast density in mammograms can obscure cancers, and raise uncertainty on the effectiveness of mammograms. In this project, statistical software was run on mammographic breast density, patient demographics, genetic risk assessment results, and Stavros lymph node character to determine whether breast density is an independent risk factor for breast cancer. This was a retrospective study of ultrasounds and mammograms performed at a community hospital. Using Microsoft Excel and SAS, correlation coefficients and ANOVAs were tabulated, yielding the statistically significant result that breast density is an independent risk factor for breast cancer. Work presented at Intel's International Science and Engineering Fair in 2015.
Characterizing Axillary Lymph Nodes
While abnormal lymph nodes were well described, there is not good data or description of the range of appearances of normal lymph nodes. If normal lymph nodes are well described, then it follows that ultrasound detection of abnormal lymph nodes would become more sensitive and specific. This could be advantageous in looking for early involvement and staging of breast cancer. It could also help detect and differentiate other disease processes such as infection, inflammation, or other neoplastic conditions such as lymphoma. This retrospective study correlates and conducts ANOVAs on patient demographics and Stavros lymph node character. This should improve sensitivity and specificity of staging breast cancer and also reduce the number of unnecessary biopsies. Work presented at Intel's International Science and Engineering Fair in 2014.

Phenotyping Alzheimer's with Electronic Health Records
DESCRIPTION Alzheimer's disease is a chronic neurodegenerative disease associated with of 60–70% of cases of dementia. The cause of Alzheimer's disease is poorly understood as are the disease subtypes. What risk factors exist for Alzheimer’s and are its sequelae are recorded in electronic health records? Do they vary by age of Alzheimer’s disease onset? Gender? Using the Foundation for Precision Medicine's Electronic Health Record data, this work seeks to better characterize Alzheimer’s disease subtypes using unsupervised machine learning methods.
NASA CAMS
DESCRIPTION Meteors are being tracked through the sky by the CAMS system around the world. However, many of the "meteors" picked up by this system are false positives -- it also detects birds, clouds, and planes without distringuishing between them. In the past, manual inspection was the way to purge these falsities. In this project, a Python script was developed to automate the process of tracking meteor showers associated with long period comets by extracting data from an FTP server, running computer vision analysis, updating logs, using deep-learning-based classification (for ridding the dataset of false positives), and upload cleaned data for visualization.
